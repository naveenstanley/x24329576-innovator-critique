# The Fox of Glendalough

In the mossy ruins of Glendalough, where Ireland's ancient round tower pierced the pewter sky like a wizard's staff, a red fox named Saoirse discovered a forgotten orchard of wild green apples — and beside it, a crumbling stone circle where, legend held, a druid sorcerer once taught the animals to still their minds. Each dawn, Saoirse would pad through the wet ferns, bite into a tart green apple so the sour shock would anchor her to the present moment, then settle onto the flattest stone and close her amber eyes, her breath slowing until even the wrens perched on her ears without startling her. The local farmers noticed something strange: their anxious border collies began following the fox to the circle, then their skittish horses, then their children — and soon a peculiar pilgrimage of creatures and humans sat together in silence each morning, the wizard's old magic alive again not as spell-craft but as the simple, radical act of being still together in a wild place. The apples grew sweeter that year, or perhaps everyone just tasted them more carefully, and Saoirse — who had once been the most hunted animal in the valley — became its most trusted teacher, proving that peace is not the absence of a predator but the presence of attention.
