# Problem Statement

## The Silent Crisis: Untreated Mental Health in Irish Farming Communities

### The Problem

Irish farmers experience chronic stress, anxiety, and depression at rates significantly exceeding the general population — yet they are among the least likely demographics in Ireland to seek professional mental health support.

**Key findings from research:**

- Approximately 60% of Irish farmers report experiencing feelings of isolation, intensifying stress and mental health struggles.
- A 2019 Empathy Research/AgriLand survey found 57% of Irish farmers were impacted by anxiety and 46% by depression.
- Farmers who sustain serious injuries are 40% more likely to experience depression and anxiety symptoms.
- Over 80% of UK/Irish farmers under 40 believe poor mental health is the biggest hidden problem in agriculture.
- Irish farmers work some of the longest hours of any occupation, often exceeding 80 hours/week in peak seasons.
- For young farmers, the top five factors affecting wellbeing are: workload/socialising time, financial concern, isolation/loneliness, family pressures, and succession stress.

### Why Existing Solutions Fail This Segment

1. **Cultural stigma**: Farmers perceive mental health services as incompatible with rural identity. They associate therapy with weakness, not resilience.
2. **Lack of rural empathy**: Farmers believe healthcare professionals don't understand farming stressors. Services use clinical language and urban frameworks.
3. **Logistical barriers**: Erratic, season-dependent schedules (lambing, calving, silage) make weekly appointment-based therapy impractical. Many farms are 30+ minutes from the nearest town.
4. **Preference for informal support**: Farmers rely on family, friends, and peer networks — but these are untrained and overburdened.
5. **No nature-integrated option exists**: Despite strong evidence that nature-based mindfulness interventions produce medium-to-large therapeutic effect sizes (Cohen's d 0.54–0.90), and that wild/forest settings outperform garden/park settings, no structured programme exists that leverages the farmer's own landscape as the therapeutic environment.

### Narrow Customer Segment

**Primary segment:** Male and female Irish farmers aged 25–55, operating small-to-medium livestock or tillage farms (20–80 hectares) in counties with the highest isolation indices (West Cork, Kerry, Roscommon, Leitrim, Mayo, Donegal), who exhibit moderate stress or anxiety symptoms but have never accessed formal mental health support.

**Estimated segment size:** ~30,000–40,000 individuals across Ireland's ~135,000 active farms.

### The Opportunity

There is a validated gap between the evidence base for nature-based mindfulness therapy (which shows strong outcomes for stress, depression, anxiety, and loneliness) and the delivery model for Irish farming communities (which requires flexible scheduling, non-clinical framing, landscape-native experiences, and peer-led trust). No existing service bridges this gap.

**Sources:** ScienceDirect (Brennan et al., 2024); National Elf Service (O'Connor et al., 2025); PMC (Djernis et al., 2019); The Informed Farmer (2024); Irish Farmers' Association; Empathy Research/AgriLand (2019); Nature.com (Kim et al., 2023).
